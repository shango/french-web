# Le Cahier — Learning-Science Evidence Memo

*Internal reference. Backs the "why it works" claims on the marketing site with the actual
studies and effect sizes, and flags the one framing that would undercut credibility. Effect
sizes use Cohen's d / Hedges' g, where ~0.2 is small, ~0.5 moderate, ~0.8 large.*

---

## 1. Spaced repetition (the spacing effect)

**Claim it supports:** *"We show you each word right before you'd forget it. Spacing beats
cramming for memory that lasts — one of the most replicated findings in learning science."*

**Evidence.** Distributing study over time (spacing) reliably outperforms massing
(cramming) for verbal material. Reviews estimate the spacing effect on verbal learning at
roughly **d ≈ 0.85 (large)** (Wiseheart et al., 2019; Donoghue & Hattie, 2021). In
real-world *classroom vocabulary* specifically, a meta-analysis found a moderate benefit
of distributed over massed practice, **d ≈ 0.54, 95% CI [0.31, 0.77]**. The base literature
is enormous — Cepeda et al.'s (2006) review covered **400+ reports**.

**For our exact use case (L2 vocabulary):** Kim & Webb's (2022) meta-analysis —
**98 effect sizes across 48 experiments, N = 3,411** — found spaced practice significantly
beat massed, and that *longer* spacing beat shorter spacing at longer retention intervals.
Benefits can persist for years (Bahrick's and Cepeda et al.'s work documents retention of
Spanish/foreign vocabulary over long delays, up to ~8 years).

---

## 2. Active recall with immediate feedback (retrieval practice / testing effect)

**Claim it supports:** *"You don't re-read words, you recall them — and getting corrected
on the spot makes them stick."* (This is our tap-to-answer card + per-tap green/red.)

**Evidence.** Retrieving an answer from memory beats re-reading/re-studying:
**g ≈ 0.50, 95% CI [0.42, 0.58]** vs. reading (Rowland, 2014) and **g ≈ 0.61,
95% CI [0.58, 0.65]** vs. other study activities (Adesope, Trevisan & Sundararajan, 2017).
The effect holds for simple word lists through to prose. On feedback timing, Kim & Webb
(2022) found **large effects of immediate feedback (g ≈ 1.04, 95% CI [0.59, 1.49])** for L2
vocabulary retention — support for correcting the learner the instant they tap.

## 3. Thematic (situational) grouping — and the terminology landmine

**Claim it supports:** *"Nouns come grouped by where you'll actually use them — the
restaurant, the café, the taxi — which is learned faster and remembered better than random
lists, or than lists of same-category words."*

**The landmine (read this).** In applied linguistics, **"lexical set" = "semantic
cluster"**: words of the *same category and word class* (e.g., glass, cup, saucer, plate;
eye, nose, ear, mouth). The classic evidence says teaching *new* L2 words this way
**hinders** learning, via interference theory — similar items compete and cross-associate:

- Tinkham (1993, *System*): new words in semantic clusters were learned **more slowly than
  semantically *unrelated* (random) words**.
- Tinkham (1997, *Second Language Research*) and Waring (1997) replicated the hindrance;
  Erten & Tekin (2008, *System*) and Finkbeiner & Nicol (2003) add support.

So marketing this as **"lexical sets" would name the technique that backfires.** Don't.

**What we actually do is the good kind.** Grouping by *situation/scenario* (restaurant:
waiter, menu, order, bill) is **thematic clustering** — words linked by context, often
across word classes. Tinkham (1997) found semantic clustering hinders while **thematic
clustering facilitates** L2 vocabulary development; later studies report the thematic group
as clearly superior in learning and retention. Thematic grouping beats **both** random
lists and same-category sets.

**Honesty caveat (keep in your back pocket).** Recent results on *semantic* sets are mixed
rather than uniformly negative — some studies find no significant difference, and a
"desirable difficulties" reading (Bjork, 1999) argues the early interference can aid
long-term retention. This doesn't change our story (we use thematic, not semantic), but if
challenged, the honest line is: *thematic grouping is well-supported; semantic-set effects
are debated.*

---

## 4. The cognate windfall (context, not a "technique")

The ~one-third-of-English-from-French figure is a **corpus/etymology fact**, not a learning
intervention — and cognates genuinely lower the learning burden for English speakers. Frame
it as a real head-start, not as "proven learning tech," and keep the false-friend caveat
(*pain* = bread) so it stays honest.

---

## Do / don't for copy

- **Do** say: spaced repetition, active recall, immediate feedback, thematic / situational
  grouping; "one of the most replicated findings in learning science"; cite that spacing
  beats cramming and thematic beats random and same-category lists.
- **Don't** say: "lexical sets," "semantic sets," "learn 3× / 200% faster," or any single
  invented multiplier. There isn't a clean headline number, and a fabricated one is the
  easiest thing for a skeptic to disprove — which would cost more than it's worth given the
  brand runs on honesty.
- **If you want a number in copy,** quote an effect size in plain terms ("a large, well-
  replicated effect") rather than a percentage.
